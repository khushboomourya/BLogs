In construction jobs, working at heights —whether on scaffolding, rooftops, or ladders is unavoidable. One of the leading causes of workplace injuries and fatalities is falling from a height. It has been reported that falling from heights accounts for more than 30% of construction-related deaths. According to a study in ScienceDirect.com, falls from roofs, ladders, and scaffolding lead to approximately 28.5% of fatal work-related events in the construction industry.

Fall protection systems help prevent such incidents. This equipment is not optional; it is legally mandated and critical for jobsite safety.

If you are new to construction or purchasing fall protection equipment for the first time, this guide will help you make the right choice.

What are Fall Protection Solutions?

Fall protection solutions combine safety equipment and engineered systems that help safeguard or arrest falls while working at height. These are regulated by national and international standards, such as:

  • EN
  • IS
  • ANSI
  • AS/NZS

Fall protection solutions include:

  • Full body harness
  • Lanyards (with or without shock absorbers)
  • Retractable Fall Arresters (Retractable Blocks)
  • Anchorage Devices (fixed or mobile)
  • Connectors (karabiners, hooks, etc.)

These work together as an integrated personal fall arrest system or fall restraint system, depending on the task.

Full Body Harness: Your Core Safety Wear

What is a Harness?

A full body harness is a safety device designed to distribute fall forces across key muscle groups, including shoulders, thighs, chest, and pelvis, during a fall. It is an upgraded version of outdated body belts and an essential part of your fall arrest system.

Common Parts of a Full Body Harness

  • Shoulder Straps
  • Chest Straps
  • Thigh Straps
  • Waist Straps
  • Dorsal D-ring (for fall arrest)
  • Lateral D-rings (for work positioning)
  • Sternal/Front D-rings (for climbing or rescue)
  • Ventral D-ring

When choosing a full-body harness,

  • Ensure compliance with IS 3521:2021, EN 361, or ANSI Z359.11.
  • The fit of the harness is important. The straps should be snug but not restrictive.
  • Make sure you inspect before each use.

Lanyards: The Connecting Safety Line

What is a Lanyard?

A lanyard is a flexible connecting device that connects the harness to an anchorage point or retractable device. It typically contains a shock absorber to reduce the force of a fall arrest.

Types of Lanyards

  • Shock-absorbing Lanyard- For general fall arrest
  • Restraint Lanyards- To prevent reaching the edges
  • Forked/Twin-leg Lanyards- For continuous tie-off
  • Sharp-edge Tested Lanyards- Ideally used on steel beams or edges
  • Work Positioning Lanyards- For stability and support for working at heights

Some Tips While Choosing a Lanyard

  • The standard length ranges between 1.5 to 2 meters.
  • Use twin-leg lanyards to maintain 100% tie-off during transitions.
  • Should comply with industry standards, including EN 354:2010, EN 355:2002, and IS3521:Part 2-2021.

Retractable Blocks: Controlled Fall Arrest Devices

What are Retractable Blocks?

Retractable blocks are also called self-retracting lifelines (SRLs) or retractable fall arresters. These are mechanical fall arrest devices with an internal locking system. These automatically extend and retract the lifeline with user movements, and lock instantly in the event of a fall.

There are various specialized retractable blocks, such as retrieval blocks that offer a dual-function system (fall arrest and winch).

Components of a Retractable Block

  • Housing (Rugged shell that is usually made from polymer/metal and protects the internal mechanism)
  • Cable or Webbing Lifeline
  • Swivel Snap Hook
  • Locking Mechanism

Advantages of Retractable Blocks

  • Allows free movement across a work area.
  • Minimizes fall distance and impact
  • Minimizes trip hazards compared to fixed-length lanyards.
  • Can be easily installed and removed.

Where to Use Retractable Blocks?

  • Scaffold Work
  • Warehouses and Mezzanines
  • Cranes or Rooftop Maintenance
  • Vertical Climbing Applications (when used with an overhead anchor)

Note: Do not use a retractable block horizontally unless it is edge-tested and rated for horizontal use. Check the manufacturer’s label before use.

Anchorages: The Foundation of Fall Protection

What is Anchorage?

An anchorage is a secure attachment point in a personal fall protection system. It connects to structures, such as a beam, rooftop, or wall, and supports the forces generated during a fall. The functioning of even an advanced harness or lanyard relies on anchorages.

Ensure that the anchorages you use are certified, structurally sound, and can withstand a minimum static force of 12kN for 3 minutes for fall arrest applications.

Common Types of Anchorages

  • Webbing anchors (Temporary)
  • Steel Anchors
  • Beam Clamps
  • Type C Horizontal Anchors (for moving systems)

Connectors: Hooks and Karabiners for Safe Linkage

What are Connectors?

Connectors, such as hooks and karabiners, are used to join harnesses, lanyards, blocks, and anchors safely.

Common Types of Connectors

  • Hooks
  • Karabiners

Best Practice

  • Use only locking types, such as screw, double, or triple lock.
  • Inspect for cracks, deformation, or corrosion before each use.

Accessories: Enhancing Safety and Comfort

These are some useful add-ons, such as:

  • Tool lanyards to safely carry tools
  • Positioning belts for hands-free work
  • Suspension Intolerance Strap and rope grabs

Accessories help enhance efficiency and minimize risk, especially in high-rise or confined spaces.

Additional Tips for Construction Workers

  • Ensure the harness straps are tightly fit and comfortable.
  • Inspect your equipment daily. Look for frayed webbing, rust, and label damage.
  • Your anchor must be certified, tested, and should meet industry standards.
  • Effective fall protection depends on proper usage rather than just having the right gear. Therefore, train properly.
  • Maintain an inspection register and remove any equipment after a fall.

How to Choose the Right Fall Protection Solution for a Construction Site?

Choosing the right fall protection tools depends on the nature of the task, the site layout, and the movement required.

  1. Assess the Site Conditions
    • Inquire whether the workers are operating at height (scaffolding or rooftops)
    • Ask if there are open edges, floor openings, or vertical climbing needs.
    • Check if movement is limited or if the worker needs to travel across spans.

    For example, on a multi-level steel frame under construction, workers would require vertical and horizontal mobility with a 100% tie-off. Here, a retractable block, twin-leg lanyard, and mobile anchor points are a must.

  2. Match the Task with the Right Equipment
    3. Task Type Recommended Solutions
    Static Work at Height (for example, rooftop, tiling, HVAC install) Full Body Harness, Shock Absorbing Lanyard, Fixed Anchor
    Frequent Movement (for example, scaffolding, rebar work) Full Body Harness, Retractable Fall Arrester, Beam Anchor
    Edge Work or Sloped Roofs Harness with front D-ring, Guided Type Fall Arrester on Rope
    Working Between Anchor Points Forked/Twin-Leg Lanyard for 100% Tie-Off
    Confined Space Entry (For example, manholes, tanks) Rescue-rated Harness, Tripod/Davit, Fall Arrest Blocks
  3. Consider Movement and Fall Distance
    • Use shock-absorbing lanyards for limited movement and high fall clearance.
    • For wide movement and low fall clearance, use retractable blocks with shorter arrest distances.
    • For vertical climbs, use vertical fall arrest systems with guided fall arresters.

    Tip: Retractable blocks minimize free fall and are ideal for places where fall clearance is less than 4 meters.

  4. Verify Anchor Point Suitability

    5. Make sure the anchor points are:

    • Rated for 12kN or more
    • Installed on stable structures
    • Positioned above the user’s back D-ring whenever possible

    Use:

    • Beam anchors for steel structures
    • Webbing anchors for temporary work
    • Tripods/Davits for confined spaces
  5. Consider Ease of Use and Worker Comfort
    • For long-duration use, go for padded harnesses.
    • Use quick-connect buckles for easy donning.
    • Choose a suitable length of retractable blocks according to the height.
    • For wide anchoring points, use scaffold hooks.
  6. Ensure Compatibility
    • Your hooks and karabiners should match the anchor and device dimensions.
    • Do not mix components from different systems unless compatible.
    • Use EN/IS/ANSI-certified products only.
  7. Always Train Before You Tie-Off

    8. If misused, even the best gear wouldn’t help. Ensure every worker knows:

    • How to wear and inspect a harness.
    • How to anchor correctly.
    • What to do in the event of a fall.
